# 96 - Director Round

## Introduction

The Director round evaluates strategic thinking, organizational leadership, budget management, and executive presence. Director-level roles involve setting technical strategy across multiple teams, managing managers, resource allocation, vendor relationships, and aligning technology with business objectives. This round tests your ability to think beyond individual teams and drive organizational impact.

This guide covers strategic planning, budget management, organizational design, vendor management, executive communication, industry trends, risk management, and P&L understanding.

---

## Theory Notes

### Director Responsibilities
1. **Strategic Planning**: Set technical vision aligned with business goals
2. **Organizational Design**: Structure teams for maximum effectiveness
3. **Budget Management**: Allocate resources across teams
4. **Vendor Management**: Evaluate and manage external partnerships
5. **Executive Communication**: Translate between technical and business
6. **Risk Management**: Identify and mitigate organizational risks
7. **Talent Strategy**: Hire, develop, and retain senior talent

### Strategic Planning Framework

#### OKRs (Objectives and Key Results)
```
Objective: Improve platform reliability
  KR1: Reduce P0 incidents from 5/month to 1/month
  KR2: Achieve 99.99% uptime
  KR3: Mean time to recovery under 15 minutes
```

#### Strategic Planning Process
1. **Assess current state**: Where are we today?
2. **Define future state**: Where do we need to be?
3. **Identify gaps**: What's missing?
4. **Prioritize initiatives**: What moves the needle most?
5. **Allocate resources**: People, budget, time
6. **Execute and measure**: Track progress, adjust

### Budget Management

#### Budget Categories
- **Headcount**: Salaries, benefits, recruiting
- **Infrastructure**: Cloud, hosting, tools
- **Software**: Licenses, subscriptions
- **Training**: Conferences, courses, certifications
- **Contingency**: Unexpected costs (10-15%)

#### ROI Calculation
```
ROI = (Benefits - Costs) / Costs × 100%
Payback Period = Investment / Annual Savings
TCO = Purchase Price + Operating Costs + Maintenance
```

### Organizational Design

#### Team Topologies
- **Stream-aligned teams**: Aligned to business flow
- **Platform teams**: Provide internal services
- **Enabling teams**: Help other teams adopt practices
- **Complicated-subsystem teams**: Handle specialized components

#### Spine Model
- **Business**: Revenue, market, customers
- **Product**: Features, UX, roadmap
- **Engineering**: Architecture, delivery, quality
- **Operations**: Infrastructure, reliability, security

### Vendor Management

#### Evaluation Criteria
1. **Technical fit**: Does it meet requirements?
2. **Cost**: Total cost of ownership
3. **Vendor stability**: Financial health, market position
4. **Support**: Quality and responsiveness
5. **Integration**: How well does it integrate with existing systems?
6. **Scalability**: Can it grow with us?

### Executive Communication

#### The BLUF Method (Bottom Line Up Front)
1. Lead with the recommendation or conclusion
2. Provide context briefly
3. Support with data
4. State the ask or next steps

## Key Concepts

### Industry Trends
- **AI/ML Integration**: Embedding intelligence across products
- **Cloud-Native**: Kubernetes, serverless, microservices
- **Platform Engineering**: Internal developer platforms
- **Developer Experience**: Tools and workflows for productivity
- **Sustainability**: Green computing, carbon-aware engineering

### Risk Management

#### Risk Assessment Matrix
| | Low Impact | High Impact |
|---|-----------|-------------|
| **High Probability** | Mitigate | Avoid |
| **Low Probability** | Accept | Transfer |

#### Common Technical Risks
- **Security breaches**: Implement defense in depth
- **Scalability limits**: Plan for growth, load test
- **Vendor lock-in**: Evaluate exit strategies
- **Key person risk**: Document knowledge, cross-train
- **Technical debt**: Allocate capacity for reduction

### P&L Understanding
- **Revenue**: How the company makes money
- **COGS**: Direct costs of delivering the product
- **Gross Margin**: Revenue minus COGS
- **Operating Expenses**: R&D, sales, marketing, G&A
- **EBITDA**: Earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, amortization

## FAQ (20+ Q&A)

### Q1: How do you set technical strategy?
**A:** Align with business objectives, assess current state, define future vision, identify gaps, prioritize initiatives, allocate resources, and measure progress through OKRs.

### Q2: How do you manage a budget across multiple teams?
**A:** Assess team needs, align with priorities, allocate based on ROI, track spending regularly, adjust quarterly, and maintain contingency reserves.

### Q3: How do you evaluate build vs buy decisions?
**A:** Consider: cost (TCO), time to market, strategic importance, team capability, maintenance burden, and vendor risk. Buy for commodity; build for differentiation.

### Q4: How do you handle organizational restructuring?
**A:** Assess current structure against goals, involve stakeholders, communicate transparently, plan transition carefully, and measure impact.

### Q5: How do you present to the C-suite?
**A:** BLUF method — lead with the conclusion, use business language, focus on impact, be concise, and have data ready.

### Q6: How do you manage vendor relationships?
**A:** Set clear SLAs, regular business reviews, diversify critical vendors, negotiate favorable terms, and maintain exit strategies.

### Q7: How do you handle competing priorities across teams?
**A:** Align with business objectives, assess impact and urgency, communicate trade-offs, make data-driven decisions, and be transparent.

### Q8: How do you build a technical vision?
**A:** Understand business direction, assess industry trends, evaluate current capabilities, define aspirational goals, and communicate the path forward.

### Q9: How do you manage risk?
**A:** Identify risks, assess probability and impact, develop mitigation strategies, monitor regularly, and communicate proactively.

### Q10: How do you handle a P&L responsibility?
**A:** Understand revenue drivers, manage costs, invest in high-ROI initiatives, track metrics regularly, and make data-driven decisions.

### Q11: How do you handle a critical production incident as a director?
**A:** Ensure the team is responding, provide support, communicate with leadership, focus on resolution, and lead the post-mortem.

### Q12: How do you foster engineering culture?
**A:** Set clear values, lead by example, recognize great work, invest in community, and create psychological safety.

### Q13: How do you handle cross-organizational dependencies?
**A:** Establish clear interfaces, regular leadership syncs, shared roadmaps, and collaborative planning.

### Q14: How do you make hiring decisions at scale?
**A:** Define hiring criteria, ensure consistent processes, diversify pipelines, invest in employer brand, and streamline interview processes.

### Q15: How do you handle tech debt at the organizational level?
**A:** Track and categorize debt, allocate budget, prioritize by business impact, communicate the case for investment, and measure improvement.

### Q16: How do you handle a failed initiative?
**A:** Conduct blameless post-mortem, document learnings, adjust strategy, communicate transparently, and apply lessons to future initiatives.

### Q17: How do you balance innovation with stability?
**A:** Allocate capacity for both (e.g., 70% core work, 20% tech debt, 10% innovation), and protect innovation time.

### Q18: How do you manage up to a CTO/VP?
**A:** Regular updates, lead with outcomes, provide options, be transparent about risks, and align with their priorities.

### Q19: How do you handle a merger/acquisition from a technology perspective?
**A:** Assess technical alignment, plan integration carefully, prioritize critical systems, manage cultural integration, and maintain business continuity.

### Q20: How do you stay current with industry trends?
**A:** Conferences, industry reports, peer networks, continuous learning, and experimentation with new technologies.

### The Three Horizons Model
- **Horizon 1**: Core business — optimize and defend (70% of resources)
- **Horizon 2**: Emerging opportunities — expand and build (20% of resources)
- **Horizon 3**: Future options — experiment and explore (10% of resources)

### The Cynefin Framework for Decision-Making
- **Simple/Clear**: Best practices apply → Sense, Categorize, Respond
- **Complicated**: Expert analysis needed → Sense, Analyze, Respond
- **Complex**: Emergent practices → Probe, Sense, Respond
- **Chaotic**: Novel practices → Act, Sense, Respond

### The Wardley Map (Strategic Visualization)
Visualize your technology landscape:
1. Map user needs
2. Map value chain
3. Map evolution stage
4. Identify strategic plays

- **Strategy**: Direction and competitive advantage
- **Structure**: How teams are organized
- **Systems**: Daily processes and tools
- **Shared Values**: Core culture
- **Style**: Leadership approach
- **Staff**: People and capabilities
- **Skills**: Organizational competencies

## Advanced Director Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Building a Multi-Year Technology Strategy

**Framework:**

```
Year 1: Foundation
- Platform modernization
- Team structure optimization
- Key hires (staff engineers, architects)
- Establish engineering culture

Year 2: Scale
- Horizontal scaling of teams
- Advanced observability
- Developer experience improvements
- Cross-team collaboration patterns

Year 3: Differentiation
- AI/ML integration
- Competitive technical advantages
- Industry thought leadership
- Open-source contributions
```

**Communication to C-suite:**
"Our technology strategy aligns with the business goal of expanding to 3 new markets. Year 1 focuses on building a scalable foundation, Year 2 on rapid growth support, and Year 3 on technical differentiation. The estimated investment is $X over 3 years, with expected ROI of Y through reduced operational costs and faster time-to-market."

### Scenario 2: Managing Across Multiple Teams with Conflicting Priorities

**Decision Framework:**
1. Align all priorities with company OKRs
2. Score each initiative on: impact, urgency, effort, risk
3. Present trade-offs transparently
4. Make decisions based on data, not politics
5. Communicate the "why" behind decisions
6. Revisit quarterly as priorities shift

**Example decision matrix:**
```
| Initiative | Impact | Urgency | Effort | Score |
|-----------|--------|---------|--------|-------|
| A | 9 | 8 | 5 | 7.3 |
| B | 7 | 9 | 3 | 6.3 |
| C | 8 | 5 | 7 | 6.7 |
```

### Scenario 3: Leading a Major Incident Post-Mortem

**As Director, your role:**
1. Ensure the team has resources to resolve
2. Communicate with executive leadership
3. After resolution, lead blameless post-mortem
4. Drive systemic improvements
5. Communicate learnings across organization

**Post-mortem template:**
```
Incident: [Description]
Duration: [Start time] - [End time] (X hours)
Impact: [Users affected, revenue impact]
Root cause: [Technical explanation]
Timeline: [Key events]
What went well: [Response aspects]
What could be improved: [Process gaps]
Action items: [Specific, owned, dated]
Systemic changes: [Process/tool improvements]
```

### Scenario 4: Vendor Consolidation Strategy

**When you inherit multiple overlapping tools:**

1. **Audit**: List all tools, costs, usage, overlap
2. **Categorize**: Must keep, could consolidate, can eliminate
3. **Evaluate**: For each consolidation candidate, assess:
   - Migration cost and risk
   - User adoption challenges
   - Cost savings
   - Capability gaps
4. **Plan**: Phased migration with clear milestones
5. **Execute**: Migrate, train, decommission
6. **Measure**: Track savings, adoption, satisfaction

**ROI example:**
"We currently spend $500K/year on 3 overlapping monitoring tools. Consolidating to one would cost $150K for migration and training, save $300K/year ongoing, and reduce context-switching for engineers."

### Scenario 5: Managing Up to a New CTO

**When a new CTO joins:**
1. Proactively schedule a 1:1
2. Prepare a concise overview of your organization
3. Share key metrics, challenges, and wins
4. Ask about their priorities and expectations
5. Offer to be a resource for understanding the team
6. Don't over-promise — be honest about current state
7. Be patient while they learn the organization

### Scenario 6: Handling an Engineering Exodus

**When key engineers are leaving:**

1. **Understand root causes**: Exit interviews, surveys, 1:1s
2. **Immediate**: Knowledge transfer, documentation
3. **Short-term**: Adjust workloads, prioritize ruthlessly
4. **Medium-term**: Hire replacements, develop internal talent
5. **Long-term**: Address systemic issues (comp, culture, growth)

**Communication to leadership:**
"Three senior engineers are departing over the next month. Here's the impact on our roadmap: [specific projects affected]. Here's our mitigation plan: [actions]. Here's what we're doing to prevent this: [systemic changes]."

### Scenario 7: Build vs Buy Decision at Scale

**Decision framework:**
```
BUILD if:
- Core differentiator
- Team has expertise
- Specific requirements not met by vendors
- Long-term cost favors building
- Strategic control needed

BUY if:
- Commodity capability
- Speed to market critical
- Team lacks expertise
- Vendor solution is mature
- Total cost of ownership favors buying
```

**Communication example:**
"I recommend we buy an observability platform rather than build one. While building gives us more control, the build cost is $2M over 3 years vs $800K for a vendor. More importantly, our engineers should focus on our core product, not internal tooling. The vendor also provides features we'd take 2 years to build."
