# Hiring Manager Round

The hiring manager round is often the most decisive interview. This person will be your direct manager if hired, and they're evaluating whether you can do the job, fit the team, and help them succeed.

## Introduction

The hiring manager round is where your candidacy often succeeds or fails. Unlike HR or peer interviews, this conversation is deeply focused on role fit, team dynamics, and whether you can solve the problems this manager is facing.

Hiring managers are busy. They're interviewing because they have a gap to fill and work that needs doing. Show them you're the person who can relieve their pain, and you'll stand out.

## Key Concepts

### What the Hiring Manager Cares About

**Can you do the job?**
- Do you have the skills and experience?
- Can you ramp up quickly?
- Can you work independently?

**Will you fit the team?**
- How will you collaborate with existing members?
- Will you add to the team culture?
- Can you work with the team's working style?

**Will you help me succeed?**
- Can you take ownership and run with it?
- Will you make my job easier or harder?
- Can I trust you with important work?

**Will you stay and grow?**
- Is this role aligned with your career goals?
- Will you be engaged long-term?
- Are you looking for growth or just a job?

### The Hiring Manager's Mindset

- **Pain-driven** — They're hiring because something hurts
- **Time-constrained** — They can't afford a bad hire
- **Risk-averse** — A wrong hire sets the team back months
- **Outcome-focused** — They want someone who delivers results

### Key Questions to Answer

- "Why do you want this job?" (Not just "why this company?")
- "What would you do in the first 30/60/90 days?"
- "What's your management style?" (and does it match theirs?)
- "How would you handle [specific team challenge]?"
- "What do you need from me to succeed?"

### The 30-60-90 Day Plan

**First 30 Days: Learn**
- Understand the codebase, processes, and team dynamics
- Build relationships with key stakeholders
- Identify quick wins and build credibility
- Shadow team members and understand workflows

**Days 31-60: Contribute**
- Take on independent tasks and deliver results
- Start proposing improvements based on observation
- Deepen relationships and trust
- Identify areas where your unique skills add value

**Days 61-90: Lead**
- Drive initiatives independently
- Mentor or guide others where possible
- Contribute to strategy and planning
- Demonstrate ownership and long-term thinking

### Questions to Ask the Hiring Manager

- What does success look like in this role at 6 months?
- What's the biggest challenge the team is facing right now?
- How would you describe your management style?
- What's the team's biggest gap that this hire would fill?
- How does the team make decisions and handle disagreements?
- What's the career growth path for this role?

## FAQ

**Q: How specific should my 30-60-90 day plan be?**
A: Be specific enough to show you understand the role, but flexible enough to show you'll adapt to what you learn.

**Q: Should I ask about the hiring manager's challenges?**
A: Yes. Showing you care about their pain points demonstrates empathy and strategic thinking.

**Q: What if the role isn't exactly what I expected?**
A: Be honest about your reaction. "That's interesting — here's how my experience aligns..." shows adaptability.

**Q: How do I handle the "why do you want this job?" question?**
A: Connect your career goals to their specific needs. Show you've thought about how you'll contribute, not just what you'll gain.

**Q: Should I negotiate in this round?**
A: It's usually better to wait for the offer stage. Focus on demonstrating fit first.

**Q: What if I'm nervous about meeting my potential manager?**
A: Remember they're also evaluating whether they want to work with you. It's a two-way street.

**Q: How do I show I can handle the role's challenges?**
A: Use specific examples from your past that directly relate to their stated challenges.

**Q: Should I bring up concerns about the role?**
A: It's okay to ask clarifying questions, but frame them as "help me understand" rather than "I'm worried about."

**Q: What if the hiring manager seems disengaged?**
A: Don't take it personally. They may be having a bad day. Focus on delivering your best answers.

**Q: How do I close the conversation effectively?**
A: Ask about next steps, reiterate your enthusiasm, and thank them for their time.

## Common Mistakes

- **Not knowing the role deeply** — Read the job description multiple times and research the team
- **Being generic** — Tailor everything to this specific role and team
- **Not asking questions** — It signals lack of genuine interest
- **Focusing only on what you'll gain** — Show what you'll contribute
- **Being overly rigid about your 30-60-90 plan** — Show flexibility
- **Not connecting your experience to their needs** — Bridge the gap explicitly
- **Forgetting it's a two-way evaluation** — You're also deciding if this manager is right for you

## Best Practices

1. **Research the team** — Understand their projects, challenges, and culture
2. **Prepare role-specific stories** — Show direct relevance to their needs
3. **Develop a thoughtful 30-60-90 plan** — Based on your understanding of the role
4. **Ask genuine questions** — About challenges, culture, and expectations
5. **Show enthusiasm** — Genuine excitement about this specific role and team
6. **Be honest about fit** — If it's not right, say so respectfully
7. **Listen to what they care about** — Then address those priorities
8. **Follow up** — A thoughtful thank-you email referencing specific conversation points
