# Flash Cards for Interview Preparation

Flash cards leverage spaced repetition and active recall to enhance memory retention of key concepts and patterns.

## Introduction

Flash cards are one of the most effective tools for active recall. Research shows active recall through flash cards is 50-100% more effective than passive review.

## Key Concepts

### Spaced Repetition Science
- Day 1: Initial learning
- Day 3: First review
- Day 7: Second review
- Day 21: Third review
- Day 60+: Maintenance reviews

### Card Types
| Type | Structure | Best For |
|------|-----------|----------|
| Basic | Question → Answer | Definitions, facts |
| Cloze | Fill in the blank | Key terms, formulas |
| Image Occlusion | Hide labels on diagrams | Visual concepts |
| Reverse | Bidirectional | Bidirectional learning |

### Design Principles
- **Atomic**: One concept per card
- **Clear**: Unambiguous question and answer
- **Contextual**: Includes enough context
- **Memorable**: Uses mnemonics or visual cues

## FAQ

### Q1: How many flashcards should I create per day?
Start with 20-30 new cards per day. Consistency matters more than volume.

### Q2: Should I make my own cards or use pre-made?
Make your own. The creation process forces you to understand the material.

### Q3: How long should daily review be?
15-30 minutes daily is sufficient. Consistency beats marathon sessions.

### Q4: What's the best flashcard tool?
Anki is the gold standard for spaced repetition.

### Q5: Should cards be simple or detailed?
Simple. One fact per card. Detail belongs in notes.

### Q6: How do I handle cards I keep getting wrong?
Simplify the card, add context, use mnemonics, or break into smaller cards.

### Q7: Should I include code in flashcards?
Minimal pseudocode or key syntax only. Focus on concepts and patterns.

### Q8: How do I prevent flashcard fatigue?
Keep sessions short, vary decks, use gamification, focus on quality over quantity.

### Q9: Should I share decks with others?
Yes. It benefits others and reviewing others' cards gives new perspectives.

### Q10: What's the most important flashcard principle?
Consistency - daily review is more important than marathon sessions.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Too many facts per card** - Break into multiple atomic cards
2. **Making cards too detailed** - Flashcards are for recall, not explanation
3. **Not using spaced repetition** - Random review is less effective
4. **Creating cards you don't understand** - Learn first, then create cards
5. **Never deleting old cards** - Prune outdated or too-easy cards
6. **Marathon review sessions** - Short daily sessions are more effective

## Best Practices

1. Atomic cards - one fact per card, no exceptions
2. Daily review - consistency beats intensity
3. Own cards - create from your own understanding
4. Use cloze deletions for key terms and formulas
5. Add images for visual cues
6. Track retention rate - aim for 85-90%
7. Prune regularly - delete outdated or mastered cards
