# English Grammar for Interviews

{: .note }
Strong grammar skills demonstrate attention to detail, clear communication, and professional competence.

## Introduction

English grammar is the system of rules governing the structure of the English language. In job interviews, strong grammar skills demonstrate attention to detail, clear communication, and professional competence.

**Why It Matters:**
- Every interview requires effective communication
- Poor grammar undermines technical expertise
- Used in written tests, emails, and verbal communication
- Professionals with strong grammar are perceived as more competent

## Key Grammar Rules

### Tenses

| Tense | Structure | Example |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| Simple Present | S + V1(s/es) | She writes reports daily. |
| Simple Past | S + V2 | He completed the project. |
| Simple Future | S + will + V1 | They will launch the product. |
| Present Continuous | S + is/am/are + V-ing | I am preparing for the interview. |
| Present Perfect | S + has/have + V3 | She has submitted the report. |
| Past Perfect | S + had + V3 | He had finished before the deadline. |

### Subject-Verb Agreement

**Rules:**
1. Singular subject → Singular verb: "The manager *reviews* the code."
2. Plural subject → Plural verb: "The managers *review* the code."
3. Compound subjects joined by "and" → Plural verb: "Tom and Jerry *are* friends."
4. Subjects joined by "or/nor" → Agree with nearer subject
5. Collective nouns → Singular verbs when acting as unit

### Articles

**Definite Article (the):** Used for specific nouns
- "The CEO approved the proposal."

**Indefinite Articles (a/an):** Used with non-specific, singular nouns
- "a" before consonant sounds: "a project"
- "an" before vowel sounds: "an email"

**Zero Article:** No article used
- Proper nouns: "Google is a tech giant."
- Uncountable nouns (general): "Information is valuable."

### Prepositions

**Common Errors:**
| Incorrect | Correct | Context |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| Discuss *about* | Discuss | "Let's discuss the project." |
| Capable *to* | Capable *of* | "She is capable of handling it." |
| Responsible *to* | Responsible *for* | "He is responsible for the budget." |

### Pronouns

**Common Errors:**
1. **Pronoun-antecedent agreement**: "Each developer should update *their* profile."
2. **Ambiguous reference**: "When the manager met the client, *he* was nervous."
3. **Who vs. Whom**: "Who" = subject; "Whom" = object
4. **Its vs. It's**: "Its" = possessive; "It's" = it is

### Modifiers

**Misplaced Modifier:**
- Incorrect: "She almost drove her kids to school every day."
- Correct: "She drove her kids to school almost every day."

**Dangling Modifier:**
- Incorrect: "Having finished the report, the printer was turned off."
- Correct: "Having finished the report, she turned off the printer."

### Parallelism

Items in a series must be in the same grammatical form.

- Incorrect: "The role requires coding, testing, and to deploy applications."
- Correct: "The role requires coding, testing, and deploying applications."

### Active and Passive Voice

**Active Voice:** Subject performs the action
- "The developer *wrote* the code."

**Passive Voice:** Subject receives the action
- "The code *was written* by the developer."

**When to Use Passive:**
- Performer is unknown or unimportant
- Emphasize the action
- Formal/scientific writing

### Direct and Indirect Speech

| Direct | Indirect |
|--------|----------|
| He said, "I am working." | He said that he was working. |
| She said, "I will submit it." | She said that she would submit it. |
| They said, "We have finished." | They said that they had finished. |

## Key Concepts

| Concept | Rule | Common Error |
|---------|------|--------------|
| Subject-Verb Agreement | Subject and verb must match in number | "The team *are* ready" |
| Articles | Use *the* for specific, *a/an* for general | "I applied for *a* position" (when specific) |
| Tense Consistency | Maintain same tense within paragraph | "She *writes* and *submitted*" |
| Modifier Placement | Place modifier next to word it modifies | "She only *eats* vegetables" |
| Parallelism | Keep list items in same form | "running, swimming, to cycle" |
| Active Voice | Prefer active for clarity | "The report *was written* by me" |
| Prepositions | Learn fixed preposition pairs | "Interested *at*" instead of *in* |
| Who vs. Whom | Who=subject, Whom=object | "The person *whom* called" |
| Its vs. It's | Its=possessive, It's=it is | "The company lost *it's* CEO" |

## Best Practices

1. **Learn basic grammar rules**: Master agreement, tenses, articles
2. **Read well-written content**: Expose yourself to correct grammar
3. **Use grammar checking tools**: Grammarly, Hemingway Editor
4. **Proofread your writing**: Check for errors before sending
5. **Practice sentence correction**: Work through exercises regularly
6. **Learn common error patterns**: Know what mistakes to avoid
7. **Write regularly**: Practice makes perfect
8. **Seek feedback**: Have others review your writing

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Impact | Solution |
|---------|--------|----------|
| Subject-verb disagreement | Unprofessional | Match subject and verb in number |
| Tense inconsistency | Confusing | Maintain same tense within paragraph |
| Dangling modifiers | Unclear meaning | Ensure modifier clearly connects to subject |
| Incorrect prepositions | Sounds wrong | Learn fixed preposition pairs |
| Parallelism errors | Awkward phrasing | Keep list items in same form |

## Quick Reference

### Grammar Rules
- Singular subjects take singular verbs
- Plural subjects take plural verbs
- Use "a" before consonant sounds, "an" before vowel sounds
- Use "the" for specific nouns
- Maintain tense consistency within paragraphs

### Commonly Confused Words
- **Its** (possessive) vs. **It's** (it is)
- **Who** (subject) vs. **Whom** (object)
- **Affect** (verb) vs. **Effect** (noun)
- **Their** (possessive) vs. **There** (location) vs. **They're** (they are)
- **Your** (possessive) vs. **You're** (you are)

### Professional Writing Tips
- Use active voice for clarity
- Keep sentences under 20 words
- Use parallel structure in lists
- Avoid double negatives
- Use correct prepositions
